Biological synapses are the junctions where neurons communicate with each other in the nervous system. The brain contains billions of neurons, and each neuron is connected to thousands of others via these synapses. When an electrical signal reaches the end of a presynaptic neuron, it triggers the release of chemical messengers called neurotransmitters. These neurotransmitters diffuse across the synaptic cleft and bind to receptors on the postsynaptic neuron, passing the signal forward.

Synaptic plasticity is the ability of synapses to strengthen or weaken over time in response to increases or decreases in their activity. This dynamic changing of connection strength is widely believed to be the cellular basis for learning and memory. The most famous rule of plasticity is Hebbian learning, which states that when two connected neurons fire together repeatedly, the synapse between them is strengthened. Conversely, if they fire out of sync, the connection weakens.

However, the brain does not learn every experience equally. A group of chemical signals called neuromodulators, including dopamine, noradrenaline, and serotonin, play a critical role in gating synaptic plasticity. When the brain experiences surprise, novelty, or a reward, neuromodulators are released globally across the cortex. These chemicals act as a licensing signal, allowing active synapses to mutate and store short-term plastic memory in the local neural circuits.

But short-term plastic memory is unstable and prone to decay. To survive long-term, memories must undergo a process called memory consolidation. During quiet wakefulness or sleep, the hippocampus replays recently stored experiences. This replay drives the selective transfer of active synapses from short-term plastic stores into the long-term stable connections of the neocortex. Once consolidated, these memories become highly resistant to noise and interference, forming the stable knowledge base of the organism.
